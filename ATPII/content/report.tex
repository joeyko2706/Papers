\chapter{Chapter 1}\label{cha:cha1}


Due to its unprecedented sensitivity in the infrared (IR) bans, the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has detected several dozens active galactic nuclei (AGNs) at high redshift $(z\sim3-11)$ with much lower bolometric luminosities than studied in previous studies of high redshift quasars with luminosities of $L_{\text{AGN}}\sim 10^{42}-10^{45}\ \text{erg s}^{-1}$~\cite{10.1093/mnras/staf359}.
Such objects are called `little red dots' (LRDs).
An important property of them is their X-ray weakness as they have been undetected at the limits of the deepest Chandra fields, with only very few exceptions, regardless of the optical classification and even when stacking signals from multiple sources in standard observed-frame energy intervals (soft, hard, and full bands).
Since the discovery of LRDs in 2024~\cite{matthee2024littlereddotsabundant}, multiple theories on the X-ray weakness have been proposed.
There are three main theories: the measurable X-ray spectra are heavily absorbed; LRDs are intrinsically X-ray weak; the AGNs were misidentified.
The last hypothesis can be ruled out as very unlikely since the same X-ray weakness has been found independently of the AGN identification method and because there are objects very clearly identified as AGNs which are extremely X-ray weak~\cite{10.1093/mnras/staf359}.
Furthermore, LRDs have broad $\text H\alpha$ emission lines, which has been proposed as misidentification, as other objects can produce similar emission lines, including strong outflows driven by star formations or supernovae.
Even though this hypothesis agrees with MIRI observations~\cite{Pérez-González_2024}, NIRSpec observations have shown characteristics of LRDs that are hard to explain with this scenario~\cite{10.1093/mnras/staf359, Yue_2024}. 
Future studies into the emission lines for large LRD samples will give more information about the contribution of outflows. \newline
